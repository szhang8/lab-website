<p>A tumor is an evolving ecosystem. The dynamic interplay between heterogeneous tumor cells and their tissue microenvironment dictates how tumors initiate, develop, and metastasize. The central theme of our research program is to explore the co-evolution between tumor cells and the tumor microenvironment (TME) during the development of therapeutic resistance and metastatic relapse. An in-depth mechanistic understanding of this multifactorial co-evolution is essential for developing precision anti-cancer treatments, such as targeted therapy and immunotherapy.</p>

<p>As a translational cancer biology laboratory, we integrate multidisciplinary, innovative approaches—including <strong>cancer biology, neuroscience, bioengineering, and computer science</strong>—to investigate the dynamic interactions between tumors and their microenvironment. We use genetic animal models to simulate clinical scenarios, examine molecular underpinnings, and conduct rationally designed pre-clinical tests of novel combinatorial therapies to overcome drug resistance and prevent or treat metastasis.</p>

<h4 id="theme-1" class="wp-block-heading">Research Theme #1 - Defining and tracking evolving tumor heterogeneity using single-cell analysis</h4>

<p>Tumors are extremely heterogeneous entities. A tumor and its microenvironment form an intricate, co-evolving ecosystem. The constant interplay between the two dictates tumor initiation, progression, and metastasis. The TME, including the primary tumor milieu and the secondary metastatic niche, is highly dynamic but spatially restrained, often co-opted by tumor cells for immune evasion and metastatic outgrowth. A mechanistic understanding of the multifactorial TME is essential for the development of precision anti-cancer treatments. Our lab focuses on resolving the spatial and cellular heterogeneity of the TME and understanding how the interplay between primary tumor cells and the TME provokes drug resistance and metastasis.</p>

<h5 class="wp-block-heading"><em><strong>How do we delineate evolving tumor heterogeneity?</strong></em></h5>

<p>Developing acquired resistance to targeted cancer therapy is one of the most significant clinical challenges. Resistance under drug selection pressure results from evolutionary adaptation to a complex and dynamic tumor microenvironment (TME). We use transgenic mouse models to map the trajectory of tumor evolution. We investigate how tumors respond to selection pressures, such as chemotherapy and targeted therapy, and how these "pillars" of modern cancer therapy reshape the tumor immune landscape and influence immunotherapy and metastasis. By employing single-cell RNA-sequencing (specifically CITE-seq) and spectral flow cytometry, we dissect the enormous heterogeneity of the TME. Through high-throughput single-cell analysis, we develop testable hypotheses on tumor immunity and create novel strategies to target the complex tumor microenvironment. Read more in <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-019-11729-1">Nat Commun. 2019</a> and our newest preprint on <a href="https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2022.03.28.485781v2">bioRxiv</a>.</p>

<h5 class="wp-block-heading"><em><strong>How can we track the patterns of evolving tumor heterogeneity?</strong></em></h5>

<p>The co-evolution between a tumor and its TME is not mediated by a handful of specific genetic mutations or epigenetic marks. Rather, it is <strong>a highly plastic and continuous transcriptome trajectory</strong>. The crosstalk between transcriptome regulators and spatially dependent extracellular signaling cues collectively regulates this co-evolution. Defining the <strong>continuous transcriptome trajectory—past, present, and future—of tumor co-evolution</strong> will lead to novel therapies that deliver more sustained control of tumor progression and metastasis.</p>

<p>Dedicated regulatory signaling circuits control tumor cell heterogeneity and diverse responses to environmental stressors. The highly redundant nature of these circuits and their dynamic, non-linear crosstalk pose major research challenges. We ask a fundamental question: If the circuit is a non-linear network, is there an identifiable trajectory that controls overall tumor immunity? A comprehensive systems biology approach to map the evolving trajectories of signaling circuits is the only way to advance our understanding of metastatic immune evasion. To tackle this, we apply multiplexed genetic perturbation approaches, such as <strong>Perturb-seq or CROP-seq</strong>, and machine-learning systems biology to investigate signaling plasticity and redundancy during tumor-TME interactions.</p>

<h3 id="theme-2" class="wp-block-heading">Research Theme #2 - Understanding tumor metastasis and adaptation</h3>

<h5 class="wp-block-heading"><em><strong>Metastatic adaptation within the microenvironment</strong></em></h5>

<p>We explore how tumor cells colonize distant tissues and epigenetically adapt to drastically different environments. We recently discovered an unexpected metabolic transcriptome shift during early metastatic colonization, a process driven by the epigenetic up-regulation of glutamate decarboxylase 1 (GAD1). This adaptive up-regulation facilitates metastatic colonization. To exploit this for treatment, we showed that repurposing the FDA-approved anti-seizure therapy vigabatrin inhibits the GAD1-mediated GABA metabolic pathway, significantly decreasing metastases in vivo (Schnepp et al., Cancer Res. 2017). We have also implemented a <em>hybrid screening approach</em>—combining RNA-profiling of temporal transcriptome changes during adaptation with a rapid forward genetic screen using a <em>Drosophila</em> model—to unbiasedly identify mechanisms driving metastatic adaptation (<a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-020-16832-2">Nat Commun. 2020</a>).</p>

<h5 class="wp-block-heading"><em><strong>Mechanisms of the primed metastatic niche in promoting progression</strong></em></h5>

<p>We employ brain tissue clearing and SMART3D imaging analysis to dissect changes in astrocytes, microglia, and blood vessels in response to metastatic colonization (Guldner et al., Sci Rep. 2015). Beyond morphological changes and angiogenesis, we examine the functional role of immune cells using conditional depletion and genetic knockout mouse models. To define niche heterogeneity, we use CITE-seq to simultaneously analyze cell surface markers and matched transcriptome changes at single-cell resolution. We focus on metastasis-induced neuroinflammation and how resident immune cells contribute to progression. Read more about our study led by graduate students in <a href="https://www.cell.com/cell/fulltext/S0092-8674(20)31305-2?rss=yes">Cell 2020</a>.</p>

<h3 id="theme-3" class="wp-block-heading">Research Theme #3 - Systemic factors and the pre-metastatic niche</h3>

<p>We have broad interests in the tumor microenvironment and metastatic niche, using <strong>functional genomics</strong>, <strong>genetic mouse models</strong>, and <strong>single-cell sequencing</strong>. Key questions we are asking include:</p>

<p>1) How do systemic factors (e.g., aging, chemotherapy, gut dysbiosis) modulate the immune surveillance system and influence the metastatic niche?<br>2) What is the role of the meningeal/lymphatic system in the immune regulation of metastasis?<br>3) How can we more effectively model the "aged" tissue microenvironment in vivo?<br>4) How can we exploit the spatial features of the tumor-immune interface to deliver drugs or imaging contrast agents more specifically?</p>
